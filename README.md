Reflection

#1 How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating user stories help with this?

Interpreting user needs begins with understanding what the user is actually trying to accomplish, not just the features they request. User stories can help with this because they frame requirements in terms of goals and outcomes rather than technical tasks. Even so, user stories are only as effective as the conversations behind them. Clear requirements, direct communication, and a shared understanding of the project is essential. Test users should be as well informed about what they are testing just as much as the development team is about what they are building. The real challenge is translating user intent into something concrete and testable, and that depends more on thoughtful analysis than on any specific magagerial framework.

#2 How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?

When I approach program development, I focus on clarity, structure, and maintainability. Agile introduces useful ideas such as iterative development and regular check-ins, but I do not view Agile as inherently superior to more traditional models. Some projects benefit from flexibility, while others require firm requirements and strong top down direction. I expect to use a mix of approaches in future work, taking what is useful from Agile without assuming it is the only valid framework. Consistent communication, realistic planning, and disciplined execution matter far more than the label attached to the process.

#3 What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?

Being a good team member in software development means contributing reliably, communicating openly, and respecting the work of others. It involves taking ownership of tasks, asking questions when something is unclear, and supporting teammates when challenges arise. Methodologies can encourage these behaviors, but they cannot replace the dedication and professionalism of the people involved. In my own work experiences, I have seen well organized systems fail because of laziness and I have seen poorly structured frameworks succeed because of dedication and motivation. Success often hinges on the quality of the people involved, not the structures applied.
